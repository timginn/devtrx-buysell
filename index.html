Where to Buy/Sell?

  • Marketplaces/Auctions
  • Website/Business Brokers
  • Private Sales
  • Flippa (marketplace + Deal Flow)
  • Latona's - one of the oldest
  • Empire Flippers - one of the widest price ranges
  • FE International - better than average at vetting
  • Quiet Light Brokerage - better than average at vetting
  • and others
Centurica (more on them later) offers a Market Watch that searches multiple brokers/marketplaces.
Process (buyers):
  • Find a site you're interested in
  • Do at least some due diligence upfront (some places let you do conditional offers, others require all due diligence upfront)
  • Contact seller and get your questions answered (make sure you have some questions)
  • Decide whether or not it's the right site for you, make offer, if accepted pay and receive site.
Process (sellers):
  • Prepare documentation (financials, operating procedures, etc.)
  • Write and post a listing
  • Answer questions, close deal, collect payment and transfer site.

Pricing

Market range: starter sites up to multi-million dollar businesses.

Generally based on net profit, 2-3 year multiple of net profit EBITA (Earnings before interest, taxes, and amortization) is common.

Exceptions exist.

Higher: long established history, very low time requirements, etc.

Lower: non-English sites, Canadian / Australian content (or non-UK/EU/USA), declining revenue, etc.

Financing

On larger sales ($100,000+) it's relatively common for sellers to offer or be amenable to seller financing.

On smaller sales, entirely cash is most common/expected.

Time to sell

Most sites seem to sell (or be unlisted) within a month or two.

Some at auction go within a week. Less than that on Flippa doesn't seem ideal.

I have seen some EmpireFlippers listings sell within hours of their weekly new listing email going out.

In general, as price goes up, time to sell goes up.

Mitigating Risk

  • Beware of fraud/chargebacks How A Russian Syndicate Scammed Us For $25K (Empire Flippers)
  • For large dollar amounts, use an escrow service. Escrow.com is common. Typically escrow fees split 50%/50% buyer/seller.

What to Look for?

  • Stability and history
  • Opportunity for improvement
  • Match between site and your skills
  • Helpful/prepared seller
Past history doesn't guarantee future success; but, it's as good an indicator as you can find.
Any improvements you can make will help reduce your payback period.
Some sites need specific skills to be successful/thrive. If you don't have them, you'd need to hire for them and account for that.

To get the most value out of a sale you need a seller who's prepared and helpful. This helps with a smooth transition and reduces the risk of getting an abandoned site.

If you're a seller, spend some time on your listing/prospectus and you'll avoid a lot of duplicate questions.

Gotchas

The usual business/investing caveats apply. (Past performance does not guarantee future success).
Outright scams do exist.
Unprofessional buyers/sellers are common (as are omissions).
  • Brokers vary widely in terms of what they do to filter out fraud/scams/errors and omissions.
  • You shouldn't trust brokerages to do so anyway (e.g. they represent the seller).
If I haven't found a problem, I tend to think I'm not looking hard enough...
  • Problem: non-unique content
  • How to Find It: Copyscape or similar
  • Beware: it won't do images (or, text hidden in minages)
  • Problem: fake traffic
  • How to Find It: Google Analytics, abnormally high bounce rate and/or countries of origin, HTTP referer, etc.
  • Beware: Sometimes people are putting hidden iframes in larger sites to inflate numbers
  • Problem: doctored revenue screenshots
  • How To Find It: Something suspicious in the screenshot, numbers not adding up, date/time on the clock, etc.
  • Beware: A live walk through/screenshare is much harder (but not impossible) to fake.
  • Problem: selling a dream
  • How to Find It: "Great potential", etc. (kind of like real estate descriptions of "Handyman's Specials")
  • Beware: if it was so easy, they probably would've done it already; things usually aren't as simple as they seem at first
PayPal recurring revenue (generally) cannot be transferred.

Due Diligence

Centurica offers a service
Verify with analytics, especially third party independent analytics (e.g. Google Analytics is more trustworthy than say server logs)
Check the publicly available data that you can:
  • whois records - check ownership
  • archive.org Wayback Machine - check history
  • Netcraft - history
  • semrush - keywords, etc.
  • server HTTP headers - check for platform, obsolete / vulnerable software

What to do after acquisition?

I like to:
  • do any quick wins specific to the site
  • Check server error logs (you'd be surprised how often problems exist but are unnoticed)
  • check Google Analytics for slow pages
  • look at Google PageSpeed Insights / Pingdom Tools reports and pick off some easy ones
  • wait and see how things go for a month or two before making drastic changes
